Things Every Maintenance Tech Should Know

Tips and advice for new maintenance techs working in the hospitality industry.

AC Calls

Most AC calls in hotels are not major problems. A lot of them are simple issues that just need patience and attention.

Before assuming the unit is broken, always check the thermostat settings first. Many times the guest accidentally changes it without realizing. Also check if the unit is actually getting power before doing anything complicated.

Taking 1 minute to check the basics first can save you 20 minutes of unnecessary work.

Toilet and Sink Calls

Clogged toilets and sinks are some of the most common maintenance calls in hospitality.

Always stay calm and never rush these calls. Most problems can be fixed quickly if you stay patient. Also make sure you double check your work before leaving the room so the guest doesn’t have to call again.

Simple calls become stressful only when they are rushed.

TV Not Working Calls

A lot of TV calls are not actual technical problems. Many of them are just small things that are easy to fix.

Always check the remote first and make sure the input is correct. Sometimes the TV looks broken but it is just on the wrong input. Also check the cables behind the TV before assuming something serious is wrong.

Knowing the basics can make you look very experienced even if you are still new.

Being Coachable

The fastest way to improve as a maintenance tech is by being coachable and willing to learn.

Every experienced technician has their own way of doing things. Instead of thinking one way is better than the other, focus on learning as much as possible from everyone you work with.

The more you listen and stay open minded, the faster you will improve.

Tracking Your Work

One of the biggest mistakes new maintenance techs make is not keeping track of what they fix.

Write down what you fix every day. This helps you remember what you learned and also helps you improve faster. Over time, you will notice patterns and common problems that happen often.

Tracking your work turns experience into real knowledge.
